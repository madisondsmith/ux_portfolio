Right Learning

By Madison Smith

In remembering how a thing was learned as a boy, the man learns how to teach his child.

For this is what it is to pass on true knowledge and understanding; to demonstrate knowing rather than to perpetuate “accidentality.” Out of the individual – in whom there exists only the inability to remember foundation and recall the source of a given idiosyncrasy – springs the ability to impress upon their children only an unexamined mind. In other words, if we only ever exist in our habitual, arbitrary, and accidental modalities, we can only ever hope to pass to our children our own mistakes instead of providing them the ground work on which to make truly unique mistakes of their own. This is tantamount to exemplifying that even a parent is susceptible to err, but more precisely, that this same parent is capable of overcoming and forfending that in which they erred; there can be held transitive hope for the child, by the child, through his own eyes; learning of how a thing was overcome for the parent, a child finds proof towards their own ability and salvation.
