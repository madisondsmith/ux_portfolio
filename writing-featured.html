Death as a Point in Time

By Madison Smith

We think of death as a point in time and then imagine that our time somehow stops thereafter.
If we are to consider the moment of death as a point among time, then the implication is that there is time to flow still after.

One example is, of course, for the world — which you have left behind.
Time flows for loved ones remembering your seemingly eternal absence;
acknowledging the use of a placeholder for nothing.

But the other is for you — the departed.
It may not be time as we currently think of it.
However, this does not mean it is not time.

Think of all the ways time is kept;
of all the ways time cannot be thought kept.

It is kept through obligations; it is kept with clocks; it is kept by others’ conception of the keeping of time; it is kept by schedules and alarms; it is kept in ways that constantly remind us that it is running out.

But what of the moments that we lose time?
Or, rather, attain it.

What of the moments of being in the throes of creativity?
What of the moments of "flow"?
What of seasonality? The way things perpetually die and grow?
Shadows in the day may cast longer;
And light's rays rebuke darkness as they dance to and fro;
Atop foliage and fauna, twilight freezes the night and the day
Until blankets of cold cloud twilight sky bringing, again, a time of gray.

These are examples of time immutable —
of a contemporary and an historic age.

So, once the moment of death occurs, instead of conceptualizing it as the halting of time, it could be helpful to think of it as the death of time running out.

It was a countdown but not to your end — to your beginning.

Accumulatively, life counts down the moments that will qualify and inform the beginning that occurs after the moment of death. Death is just an instance. The afterlife is a place out of time which has been constructed by your life.

It is why the vocation of human-beings is that of the bridge maker or pontifex. Because we must spend our life bridging the gap between this life and the next; serving others and forgetting self; forfending accidentiality by being stalwart, virtuous, and contemplative; mending the scar of fallibility; reflecting good; reflecting God.

We think of death as a point in time and then imagine that our time somehow stops thereafter.
