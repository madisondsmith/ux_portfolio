A Reflection's Obligation

By Madison Smith

When you look in the mirror, what do you see?

I mean it, really: What. Do. You. See? Do you see a face you recognize? Can you remember the last time you saw a younger reflection? Is that reflection still a part of you; what it represented? Maybe you see a familiar figure. Is it familiar because it is proportionate to the expectation of what a life-lived should yield? Maybe it is unfamiliar for the opposite reason. Does the outward abstract projection correspond to the inward concrete soul against which you weigh the likelihood of such a supposedly solid image? A carapace, the flesh bears signs of weathering – proof of life’s continuity perceived as fragmented memories. Of course it is not a dream, this reflection. We don’t need convincing of being awake. But reach up and see. These hands perceive another aspect of the reflection. For, to behold an image is not solely of the eyes just as to touch a thing that is solid is not solely of the hands because the seeing of a visible image is only one path towards knowing a thing. All paths toward knowledge beg an obligation. Even the most misguided and fruitless. What is it then? The obligation of knowing that which we see in the mirror? This ability is not unique among the elect inasmuch as we all have and know a reflection and yet we claim our experience to be solitary? Solidarity being confused for singularity. What we see in the mirror is thus fragmented; marked by duality. A piece implying the whole. It is the meaning of being created in the image of God; wholeness; goodness. We are obliged to know what we see and thus are obliged to be a reflection of goodness.

When you look in the mirror, what do you see?
